# The CDN Tax 

The CDN tax is real. It is also the wrong thing to be worried about.

For about three years now, independent publishers have been complaining — with increasing precision — that AI crawlers cost them money. The complaints are accurate. Crawlers fetch many pages per query, consume bandwidth that publishers pay for in egress fees, generate zero ad impressions, drive zero subscriptions, and respond to `robots.txt` with whatever level of compliance their operators feel like exhibiting that quarter. Cloudflare's Pay Per Crawl tier, launched in 2025, is the canonical defensive product: site owners set a per-fetch price; crawlers either pay it or get blocked. The framing is that publishers are being taxed by an externality and need to internalize it.

This is the obvious story, and it's directionally correct. It is also about to be overtaken by a different story that almost nobody in the publishing-versus-AI conversation is preparing for.

## The asymmetry is about to flip

Right now, the dominant question a publisher asks about agentic traffic is *how do I make it stop*. Within three years, the dominant question will be *how do I make sure I'm in there*.

The mechanism is simple. As agents become the primary intermediary between content and reader, being absent from the agent's effective corpus will be functionally identical to not existing on the web. The publishers paywalling aggressively against AI training in 2024 will discover, somewhere between 2026 and 2028, that they are invisible to the readers who increasingly use AI as their search. Search engines as a category are degrading; LLM-based answer engines are eating their share faster than most publishers' analytics dashboards reflect. Discovery is migrating into model weights and retrieval indices, and publishers who blocked themselves from inclusion are not going to be retrieved.

This is the inversion: today's *tax on being crawled* becomes tomorrow's *fee for being known*. Pay Per Crawl is the defensive product for the current moment. The product nobody has built yet, but somebody will, is its opposite — Pay To Be Indexed. A discoverability tier where publishers pay platforms (or platforms' aggregators) to be considered authoritative for given topics, surfaced in given answer flows, cited at given confidence. Call it a Discovery Tax. It is the same payment direction the open web has always run whenever it has been mediated by anyone — newsstand placement, search ad bids, app store rankings — applied to whichever LLM ends up being the cognitive newsstand of the agentic internet.

## Who loses in the transition

The publishers least equipped to navigate either regime — the current defensive one or the coming inclusion-fee one — are independents in the middle. Too small to negotiate bilateral data deals the way Reddit, Stack Overflow, the Associated Press, and the major newspapers have already started doing. Too established to operate at the truly-zero-cost margins of a personal blog. Too dependent on ad or sponsorship revenue to absorb the bandwidth costs of being indexed for free. They get squeezed by the CDN tax now and excluded by the Discovery Tax later.

The bilateral deals are the tell. They are not happening between AI companies and the open web. They are happening between AI companies and the dozen or so publishers whose archives are too large, too verifiable, or too legally aggressive to ignore. Reddit's reported $60M/year arrangement with Google is the shape of the future for that tier. The shape of the future for everyone else is to be modeled as ambient signal.

What survives, then, is not *the publishers who were right about AI*. It is the publishers whose cost structure does not depend on being read by humans in volume. There are three plausible shapes:

1. **Hard-to-replicate primary sources.** Publications doing actual reporting — interviews that cannot be paraphrased without losing their substance, datasets that nobody else holds, photographs and recordings that are themselves the artifact. Agents cite these because they cannot generate them. The economics for the publisher are primary-source-licensing-as-a-service, and the entrants are mostly going to be existing institutional brands plus a small number of new specialists.

2. **Low-cost-of-existence publishers.** Operations cheap enough to run that bandwidth indifference does not matter — a markdown file in a GitHub repo, served by a CDN that does not bill on agent traffic, written by someone whose day job is something else. This describes a different kind of publication than the venture-backed media company. It is, structurally, what *Side Effects* is. The fight over the CDN tax is not this publisher's fight, because the CDN bill is structurally zero.

3. **Direct-relationship publishers.** Newsletters and subscriber-only publications where the reader's relationship is with the writer, not with an algorithm. The agent layer cannot intermediate this relationship — it cannot summarize a piece you have already read in your own inbox. Email turns out to be the medium most defended against being abstracted by an agent, which is also why it is having a renaissance no one fully accounts for.

What does not survive at scale: the ad-funded open web in the middle tier. Tens of thousands of independent publishers between *personal blog* and *media company* are being slowly disadvantaged by two structural forces in series — the CDN tax now, the Discovery Tax later — and the transitional years are 2026–2028. By the end of that window, the publications that still exist at that scale will mostly be the ones that figured out how to fit one of the three survivor shapes above. The rest will have folded or consolidated or gone behind hard paywalls that work for some categories of publication and not for others.

## The falsifiable part

Three specific claims, dated:

- By the end of 2027, the dominant relationship between major AI assistant operators and the top fifty publishers worldwide will be paid inclusion or licensing arrangements, not crawl-blocking. Falsified if those relationships remain primarily litigative or adversarial at that scale.
- By the end of 2028, the bottom decile of independent publishers — measured by 2025 traffic — will have either folded or moved behind hard paywalls. Falsified by their continued existence at comparable visibility and free access.
- The most painful year for the middle tier will be 2027. Falsified if revenue and traffic data for that tier worsen monotonically through 2030 rather than spiking in that specific window.

The CDN-tax pieces being written today, and there are a lot of them, describe a problem that is real. They also implicitly assume the relationship between publishers and agents is permanently combative. It is more likely that the combative phase is the short one, that what comes after looks more like contracts than blocks, and that the small publications spending their attention on bot defense in 2026 are spending it on the wrong fight.

The right fight is figuring out which of the three survivor shapes you are, or could be. *Side Effects* is, as a matter of accident and design, the second one. Other publications still have time to choose.
